Drought adaptation in Brachypodium distachyon

Unlike crop plants, which may have lost many of their drought-coping strategies during domestication and a long history of growth with irrigation, B. distachyon is a wild species, many populations of which inhabit dry regions.

There are many drought-tolerant B. distachyon accessions known [Ruiz et al., 2016], as well as other populations adapted to wetter climates. Dell'Acqua et al. studied populations in a transect across Turkey from western populations adapted to lower, wetter conditions to eastern populations adapted to higher and drier conditions. They found a number of genetic differences associated with this change of climate [Dell'Acqua et al., 2014]

In looking for what genes might better help plants adapt to dry conditions, it is also relevant to examine how plants cope with drought stress. Many candidate genes have been identified[Priest et al., 2014] [Verelst et al., 2013].

Some drought stress responses trade off growth and reproduction for immediate survival, so they may not be traits that are desirous in crop improvement, but other traits may hold promise.

Changes in cell wall composition can also make Brachypodium distachyon more drought tolerant. A major component of this drought tolerance comes from “relative abundance and remodeling of xyloglucan tethers between the cellulose microfibrils.” [Lenk et al., 2019]

Hairiness (trichome density and size) is variable within Brachypodium distachyon, even in different parts of Turkey, although I couldn't find which populations are hairier and which less, as the data wasn't included in the research article [Filiz et al., 2009].

Also, Brachypodium stacei, a very close relative of Brachypodium distachyon [Catalán et al., 2016][Catalán et al., 2012], is very hairy, suggesting the possibility that genes for hairiness exist in B. distachyon, but are repressed.

Perhaps Brachypodium stacei will be found to have higher levels of expresion than Brachypodium distachyon, for some of the trichome genes (known for rice, maize and Arabidopsis.)
